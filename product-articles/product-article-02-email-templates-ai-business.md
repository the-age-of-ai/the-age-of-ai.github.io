### Why Your Cold Emails Aren’t Working
First, let’s diagnose the issue. Most cold emails fail for a few common reasons:
- **They’re too salesy.** You’re asking for the sale before building any rapport.
- **They’re irrelevant.** The recipient doesn’t see why your message matters to them.
- **They’re boring.** No hook, no personality—just another email to delete.

Your goal isn’t to close a deal in one message. It’s to start a conversation. That means focusing on the recipient’s needs, not yours. So how do you do that at scale without burning out? Let’s break it down with a practical strategy you can use today.

### A 3-Step Framework for Cold Emails That Get Replies
I’ve sent hundreds of cold emails over the years, and this structure consistently gets responses. It’s simple, direct, and works whether you’re pitching a service, a product, or just trying to build a connection.

#### Step 1: Personalize with a Specific Hook (1-2 Sentences)
Start with something tailored to the recipient. Mention a recent achievement, a problem they might be facing, or something specific about their business. This shows you’ve done your homework. For example:

“Hey Sarah, I noticed your recent post about scaling freelance projects—impressive growth!”

Tools like LinkedIn or their website make this easy. Spend 2 minutes researching before you write. If you’re emailing in bulk, segment your list by industry or pain point so your hook still feels relevant.

#### Step 2: Offer Value, Not a Pitch (2-3 Sentences)
Don’t jump straight to “buy my thing.” Instead, give them a reason to care. Share a quick insight, a free resource, or a question that gets them thinking. Make it about them. For instance:

“I’ve found that most freelancers struggle with pricing at that stage. I’ve got a quick tip that helped me increase rates by 20% without losing clients—would you like to hear it?”

This builds trust. You’re not selling; you’re helping. If they reply, you’ve started a dialogue.

#### Step 3: End with a Low-Pressure Ask (1 Sentence)
Close with a clear, easy next step. Don’t ask for a huge commitment—just a reply or a quick chat. Example:

“Could I send over the tip? It’ll take 2 minutes to read.”

That’s it. No hard sell, no fluff. This framework keeps your email short (under 100 words) and focused on the recipient. Now, let’s talk about scaling this without losing hours of your day.
