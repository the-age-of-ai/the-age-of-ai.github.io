But here’s the problem: writing the content is taking forever. Lesson plans, scripts, quizzes, and handouts—every piece feels like a slog. You’re stuck staring at a blank screen, or worse, rewriting the same module three times because it’s just not right. Time is slipping away, and so is your motivation.

I’ve been there. Building course content is hard, especially when you’re a solopreneur or freelancer juggling everything yourself. But here’s the good news: AI tools can cut your writing time in half while still keeping your voice and expertise front and center. I’m going to walk you through a practical process to create course content faster using AI, plus give you a few actionable tips you can use today—whether you buy a toolkit or not.

## Why Writing Course Content Feels So Hard

Let’s break this down. Course content isn’t just “writing.” It’s structuring complex ideas into bite-sized, engaging lessons. You’ve got to anticipate your students’ questions, explain concepts clearly, and keep them motivated to finish. That’s a lot of mental load. Add in the pressure of deadlines or launching to a waiting audience, and it’s no wonder you’re stuck.

AI can’t replace your expertise, but it can handle the grunt work—brainstorming, drafting, and polishing—so you can focus on teaching what you know best.

## A 3-Step Process to Write Course Content with AI

Here’s a straightforward way to use AI to speed up your workflow. I’ve used this myself to build courses and help clients do the same. You’ll need a basic AI writing tool like ChatGPT (free tier works fine for starters) or Jasper if you’ve got a budget.

### Step 1: Outline Your Course with AI Brainstorming

Start by feeding the AI a simple prompt about your course topic. For example: “Generate a 5-module outline for an online course about beginner photography.” The AI will spit out a structure with module titles, key points, and sometimes even lesson ideas. It’s not perfect, but it’s a starting point.

Tweak the outline to match your style and audience. If the AI suggests “Understanding Camera Settings” as a module, but you know your students need “How to Stop Using Auto Mode,” swap it out. The goal is to offload the initial brainstorming so you’re not starting from zero.

**Pro Tip:** Be specific in your prompts. Instead of “photography course outline,” try “photography course outline for busy moms with no prior experience.” The more context you give, the better the output.

### Step 2: Draft Lessons with AI as Your Co-Writer

Once you’ve got your outline, use AI to draft individual lessons or sections. Let’s say you’re writing a lesson on “Choosing the Right Camera.” Prompt the AI with: “Write a 300-word lesson on choosing the right camera for beginners, focusing on budget options under $500.”

You’ll get a rough draft in seconds. It won’t be perfect—AI can be generic or overly formal—but it’s a foundation. Edit it to add your personal stories, examples, or data. Maybe you’ve got a funny anecdote about buying your first cheap camera. Add that in. The AI draft saves you from the blank-page panic and gets you 70% of the way there.

**Pro Tip:** If the tone feels off, tell the AI to adjust. Add to your prompt: “Write in a friendly, conversational tone like a helpful friend.” Small tweaks make a big difference.

### Step 3: Create Supporting Materials with AI

Courses aren’t just lessons. You need quizzes, worksheets, or email follow-ups. AI can help here too. For a quiz, prompt: “Create a 5-question multiple-choice quiz on beginner camera settings with answers.” For a worksheet: “Design a one-page worksheet for students to plan their first photo shoot.”

These extras take time, and AI can churn them out fast. Review and customize them to ensure they’re useful, not just filler. Your students will notice if you skimp on quality.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid with AI Writing

AI isn’t magic. If you’re not careful, you’ll end up with generic, soulless content that bores your students. Here are two traps to dodge:

- **Over-Reliance on AI Output:** Don’t copy-paste and call it a day. Your course needs your unique perspective. Use AI as a starting point, not the final product.
- **Ignoring Your Audience:** AI doesn’t know your students like you do. If your course is for retirees learning photography, make sure the language and examples fit—not some default millennial vibe the AI might assume.

## Bonus Tip: Batch Your AI Writing Sessions

Here’s a productivity hack I swear by: batch your AI writing tasks. Set aside an hour to generate outlines for multiple modules or draft several lessons at once. Why? AI tools work best when you’re in a focused flow, iterating on prompts and refining outputs. Switching between writing and other tasks kills momentum. Batch it, edit later, and watch your progress skyrocket.
